There is a room.

It was once lived in.

You are not the one who lived here.

A cup. Ceramic. The handle is worn smooth.

Last held at dawn. Someone drank from this every morning for seven years.

A key. Iron. Too small for any door you see.

It opened a box. The box held letters. The letters were never sent.

A photograph. Edges curled. Two people. You do not know them.

Taken in this room. Thirty years ago. On a day when the light came through the window at this exact angle.

There is a door on the far wall.

It will not open today.

A low table. Worn wood. Its surface bears the marks of many arrangements — small scratches, faint rings where cups have rested, the dust of years in the grain.

This is where things may be returned. Not to where they were found. To where they belong, by your reckoning.

A ledger lies open on a shelf. The pages are bound in cloth that was once dark. The spine bears no title.

Each time the room answers, the exchange is written here — not in your hand, but in a hand that wrote before you entered. The ink is fresh. The paper is old. The earliest entries are nearly unreadable, as though centuries of water had risen from below and drowned them in their own accumulation.

You may read what has been spoken. You may watch it fade. You cannot stop the drowning.

The ledger fills itself. What you speak to the room is written here, and what the room returns is written beside it. The two accounts do not match. They never have. They never will.

The record of all that has been spoken and answered, in the order of its accumulation:

A panel on the eastern wall — or perhaps the western, you cannot quite determine which — bears the marks of repeated sound. The plaster is darker there, as if stained by voices. A small opening, like a mouth.

Speak here. The room will answer. But not with your voice. It will answer with what it remembers hearing, layered beneath what you are saying now.

You have heard yourself speak, and heard another voice beneath it — older, patient, speaking the same words in a grammar you do not recognize. The two sounds do not separate. They are woven now.

A window sill. Dust collects there in the shape of what once rested against the glass — not in drifts, but in the precise outline of an object. A vase, perhaps. Or a candle. The dust remembers better than the thing itself would have.

You may wipe this surface. When you do, the shape dissolves. What lay beneath will show itself. Some things, once erased, cannot be restored.

Your finger comes away gray. You have touched something you cannot name. The sill is empty now. It was not always.

The room remembers. What you have arranged, it held before. The dust settles in the same pattern. The weight of the absent object is still legible in the grain.

You have read the room in the grammar of yourself. The objects rest where your hand placed them, not where the room had known them. The two arrangements do not look the same. Now you see the difference.
